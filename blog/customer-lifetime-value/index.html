What Is Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)?

So, what is CLV anyway? Customer lifetime value (CLV) is a way for businesses to figure out how much money they can expect to make from a customer over the entire time they do business with them. 

In other words, CLV can be interpreted as the representation of the total value a customer brings to a business over the entire duration of their relationship.

  • For example, let’s say you have a regular customer who comes to your lemonade stand every day and spends $2 on a glass of lemonade. You estimate that this customer will continue to come to your stand until the end of next year. 

That means the total revenue you can expect to earn from this customer is $2 x 365 days = $730 and this is CLV.

  • Now, let’s compare this to an occasional customer who comes to your stand once a month and spends $3 on a glass of lemonade. You estimate that this customer will only come to your stand once for the next three months. 

That means the total revenue you can expect to earn from this customer is $3 x 3 = $9.

Through CLV, it shows that even if the occasional consumer pays more per glass of lemonade, the regular customer is worth more to your company since they buy more often and for longer.

This is why understanding the CLV of your customers is so important! It helps you identify the customers who are most valuable to your online business, so you can focus on retaining them and providing them with excellent service.

What Is The Difference Between CLV And LTV?

If you’ve heard about LTV (Lifetime Value), you may have already been familiar with the concept of understanding the long-term value of a product or customer. 

However, through this article, you may also be confused with CLV (Customer Lifetime Value), which specifically measures the value of a customer over the entire duration of their relationship with a business.

While both metrics are valuable for understanding the long-term value of investments, it’s important to understand the differences between them and how they can be used to back up strategic decisions:

  • CLV specifically measures the value of a customer, taking into account their purchasing behavior, frequency of purchases, and other factors that may influence their buying decisions.

It helps businesses identify their most valuable customers and make informed decisions about marketing, customer acquisition, and retention strategies.

  • LTV, on the other hand, is a broader metric that can be applied to a variety of things, including products, brands, or customer segments. 

It measures the total value of something over its entire lifetime, including any future earnings or cash flows. This makes it a valuable tool for businesses looking to estimate the long-term value of their investments and make strategic decisions about where to focus their resources.

How to Calculate Customer Lifetime Value?

Manual method

Calculating customer lifetime value (CLV) involves several steps, but here’s a general customer lifetime value formula that you can use:

Let’s break down each of these components:

  • Average Value of a Sale: This is the average amount of money that a customer spends per transaction. To calculate this, you can divide your total revenue by the number of transactions over a given period of time.
  • Number of Repeat Transactions: This is the number of times a customer is likely to buy from you over the course of their lifetime. To estimate this, you can look at historical data on customer retention rates or conduct surveys to ask customers how often they plan to buy from you in the future.
  • Average Retention Time: This is the average amount of time that a customer stays loyal to your business. To calculate this, you can look at historical data on customer retention rates or conduct surveys to ask customers how long they plan to keep buying from you.

Once you have these 3 pieces of information, you can plug them into the formula to calculate the CLV for each customer.

Customer Lifetime Value Examples

While it’s important to understand the theory and best practices behind increasing customer lifetime value, it can be difficult to put them into practice without real-world examples. Plus, seeing how other businesses have successfully increased their CLV can provide valuable insights and inspiration for your own efforts.

With that said, here are some real-life examples of customer lifetime value (CLV):

  1. Starbucks: Starbucks estimates that the CLV of a loyal customer is around $14,000, which is nearly three times the value of an occasional customer. This is due to the fact that loyal customers visit Starbucks more frequently, purchase more items per visit, and are more likely to try new menu items.
  2. Amazon: Amazon’s CLV varies depending on the product category, but on average, a loyal customer is worth around $1,400 over their lifetime. This is because Amazon’s loyal customers tend to make more frequent purchases, have larger order sizes, and are more likely to subscribe to services like Amazon Prime.
  3. Zappos: Zappos, the online shoe retailer, has built its business around providing exceptional customer service. Therefore, the company’s loyal customers have a CLV that is 75% higher than that of its occasional customers. This is due to the fact that loyal customers are more likely to make repeat purchases and refer their friends and family to Zappos.

These examples demonstrate the importance of customer loyalty and the impact that it can have on a business’s bottom line. By closely monitoring CLV metrics, improving the customer experience, and building strong relationships with customers, businesses can increase their CLV and drive long-term growth.

5 Tips to Increase Customer Lifetime Value

Successful CLV examples are helpful, but so are the best practices for establishing CLV in your own company. By adopting these ideas, you may get insight into your clients’ actions and preferences, improve the effectiveness of your advertising, and, eventually, grow your business. 

Here are a few ideas to think about:

  1. Focus on customer experience: Providing exceptional customer service and creating a positive customer experience can go a long way in building customer loyalty and increasing CLV. This includes things like personalized interactions, fast and efficient service, and resolving customer complaints and issues quickly.
  2. Offer loyalty programs: Loyalty programs are a great way to incentivize customers to make repeat purchases and increase their lifetime value. This can include things like offering discounts or rewards points for every purchase, or providing exclusive access to special promotions or products.
  3. Upsell and cross-sell: Encouraging customers to purchase additional products or services can help to increase their lifetime value. This can be done through upselling (offering a higher-priced product or service) or cross-selling (offering a complementary product or service).
  4. Encourage referrals: Referral programs can be a powerful tool for increasing customer loyalty and lifetime value. By offering incentives for customers to refer their friends and family, businesses can tap into the power of word-of-mouth marketing and acquire new customers at a lower cost.
  5. Monitor and analyze customer data: Tracking customer data and analyzing customer behavior can provide insights into how to improve the customer experience and increase CLV. This includes things like monitoring purchase history, analyzing customer feedback, and tracking engagement with marketing campaigns.

By implementing these best practices, businesses can improve customer loyalty, increase CLV, and drive long-term growth.
